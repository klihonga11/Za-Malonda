4.1 Introduction

This is the second step of the double entry bookkeeping process.

After entering the transaction in the general journal, the same transaction must be entered in the general ledger.

While a general journal records transactions according to the date, a general ledger shows the same transactions but according to the accounts they belong to.

The process of copying information from the journal to the ledger is called posting.

Similar to the special journals, you can also have different ledgers to record information about different types of accounts that have a high number of transactions. They are called subsidiary ledgers.

Some common subsidiary ledgers are:

  • Accounts Receivable Ledger - includes accounts of customers who owe the business money for products bought on credit.
  • Accounts Payable Ledger - includes accounts of suppliers who the business owes money for products purchased on credit.


Note: For purposes of this course, we will only record entries in the general ledger.
