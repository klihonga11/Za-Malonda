6.6 Margin

Margin (also known as profit margin) is the money the business keeps from selling a product.

Margin = selling price – cost price

You will notice that this formula is the same as when calculating a markup. However, the difference is shown when calculating margin as a percentage.

Margin = (selling price – cost price) / selling price * 100

Thomas buys one sugarcane at K 100 and sells it at K 150. Using the formula, Thomas’s margin for sugarcane is K 50 which is a margin percentage of 33.3%.

This means that for each sugarcane sold at K 150, Thomas keeps 33.3% (K 50) of the sale as profit while the remaining 66.7% (K 100) is what was spent on purchasing the sugarcane.


What would the margin on sugarcane be if Thomas bought the sugarcane at K 100 but sold it at K 250? The margin would be 60%. This means that for each sugarcane sold at K 250, Thomas keeps 60% (K 150) of the sale as profit while the remaining 40% (K 100) is what was spent on purchasing the sugarcane.

What would the margin on sugarcane be if Thomas bought the sugarcane at K 75 and sold it at K 150? The margin would be 50%. This means that for each sugarcane sold at K 150, Thomas keeps 50% (K 75) of the sale as profit while the remaining 50% (K 75) is what was spent on purchasing the sugarcane.

This shows that in order to increase your margin, you must either:

  • Sell your products at a higher price or
  • Purchase your products at a lower price


The goal of every business is to sell its products at the highest margin possible because having high margins increases the chances of reaching profitability faster.

A high margin means that the business will have a higher gross profit, which means it has more money to pay its expenses and will therefore make a higher net profit at the end of its fiscal period.

Therefore, it is easier to get people to invest in your business if the business shows that it can sell its products at high margins.

Businesses and the industries they operate in are different, but a high margin can be considered to be above 50%.


Markup will always be higher than margin. As you've seen, Thomas's markup on sugarcane is 50% while the margin is 33.3%.

When deciding on the prices of your products, it is more important to look at the margin and not the markup. This is because the markup is just how much more you are selling a product while the margin is how much you keep as profit from selling a product. Therefore, focusing on the markup can mislead you into thinking you are making more money than you actually are.

If you want to sell a product at a specific margin, the following formula will calculate the selling price at that margin:

Selling price = cost price / (1 – margin percentage)

If Thomas buys the sugarcane at K 100 but wants to make a 75% margin, using the formula provided, he will need to sell each sugarcane at K 400. The markup at this selling price is 300%.
